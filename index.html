🦠The Problem: Destructive Local Viruses

  • 🦠 Tomato Brown Rugose Fruit Virus (ToBRFV)
  • 🦠 Tomato Spotted Wilt Virus (TSWV)
  • 🦠 Tomato Mosaic Virus (ToMV)

For years, home gardeners and small growers had very few options when these viruses appeared. Beginning around 2023, new tomato hybrids became available with genetic resistance specifically targeting these devastating diseases.

The challenge: These advanced commercial varieties are usually sold only to large-scale growers in lots of 1,000 to 5,000 seeds. That makes them impractical and expensive for home gardeners and small market gardeners. Transplant-ready plants have not been available locally.

🧬Technical Description from the Plant Breeder

The following description comes from the team responsible for the genetic development of the INKA F1 hybrid.

Extremely vigorous and fast-growing plant with excellent foliage coverage, ideal for long production cycles. Strong root system with high resistance to nematodes. Short internodes with an average of 12 flowers and 10 fruits per cluster.

This hybrid is especially suited to hot climates, with genetic potential that allows it to thrive under high temperatures. It produces extra-large, firm fruit with exceptionally thick walls, intense red color, excellent flavor, and excellent shelf life.

🛡️Disease Resistance

High Genetic Resistance

  • Tomato Spotted Wilt Virus (TSWV)
  • Tomato Mosaic Virus (ToMV)
  • Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus (TYLCV)
  • Fusarium Wilt, races 1, 2, and 3
  • Root-knot nematodes
  • Verticillium Wilt

Intermediate Genetic Resistance

  • Tomato Brown Rugose Fruit Virus (ToBRFV)
  • Fusarium Crown and Root Rot
  • Leaf Mold
